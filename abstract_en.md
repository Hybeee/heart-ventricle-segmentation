Segmentation of Cardiac Structures on CT Scans

_, _, _

Introduction
Cardiovascular CT image processing plays a critical role in cardiological diagnostics and surgical planning. Accurate segmentation of the left ventricle (LV) is essential for determining functional parameters, such as ventricular volume, and for creating 3D anatomical models to support surgical decision-making. Because manual evaluation is time-consuming and subject to inter-observer variability, automated and semi-automated methods are necessary to accelerate clinical workflows.

Objectives
This study aims to develop, implement, and evaluate a robust algorithmic method for LV segmentation on contrast-enhanced CT scans. Specifically, the objective is to refine initial, over-segmented masks generated by deep learning models (e.g., nnU-Net) using a classical computer vision approach guided by anatomical priors, thereby increasing segmentation accuracy and stability.

Methods
The proposed algorithm derives a globally homogeneous threshold from stable axial slices to segment the LV. Stable slices are identified as those where the nnU-Net mask exhibits the highest volume. For each selected slice, the directional derivative relative to the mask’s center of mass is computed and resampled into polar coordinates. We observe that true boundaries naturally align with gradient valleys in this 1D polar directional gradient map. Consequently, 100 candidate thresholds are proposed and scored based on two equally weighted components: the depth-weighted average squared distance of mask boundaries along gradient valleys, and the mean radial difference between neighboring boundary points. The threshold with the minimum score is selected. To enhance 3D robustness, the algorithm evaluates multiple slices, combining their best thresholds using the weighted mean or median of reciprocal scores.

Results
The method produced accurate segmentations on high-quality images, confirming that homogeneous thresholding can effectively capture LV boundaries when guided by anatomical priors. While severe image noise (e.g., impulse noise or streaking artifacts) degrades overall performance, a natural reduction in mask stability towards the basal and apical regions is observed even in high-quality CT scans. While current 2D postprocessing reduces leakage, limitations remain in challenging cases. Qualitative 3D assessment supports the validity of the approach, though further quantitative evaluation is ongoing.

Conclusion
This work demonstrates that combining deep learning-based initialization with anatomically informed thresholding yields a promising framework for LV segmentation. Future work includes development of a full 3D postprocessing pipeline, comprehensive quantitative validation, and extension to other cardiac structures such as the right ventricle.
